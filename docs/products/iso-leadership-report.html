Leadership requested three capabilities: ISO standards mapping, explainability (trace every score to specific evidence and standards), and confidence scoring (how reliable is each score). This report summarizes the architecture, design decisions, and phased implementation plan.

Key outcome: Phase 1 delivers ISO-traceable language in exported reports (PDF/Word) with zero changes to the existing scoring pipeline. This means no regression risk to the 4 months of prompt tuning, and immediate visible value for leadership.

During discovery, we identified a significant finding: the proposed numeric confidence model (4 weighted signals producing a percentage) is not reliably achievable with current LLM technology. We've replaced it with a qualitative model (High / Medium / Low with evidence-based rationale) that is both more honest and more useful for decision-making.

03 — Leadership's Vision

Leadership outlined a clear vision for ISO integration. Here's what was requested and how our refined approach addresses each element:

Leadership Asked For

6 ISO Standards, Tiered

Tier 1: ISO 42001 + 23894. Tier 2: 22989 + 23053. Tier 3: 42005 + 42006. "Don't boil the ocean."

Our Approach

Tiered, with 23894 as Gap-Filler

Same tiers, but ISO 23894 is supplementary to 42001 (fills risk management gaps) rather than a standalone framework. Simplifies architecture and avoids duplication.

Leadership Asked For

Confidence Scoring (Numeric %)

4 weighted signals: Evidence Specificity (35%), Coverage Completeness (30%), Response Consistency (20%), Verifiability (15%). Output: percentage like "72%".

Our Approach

Qualitative Confidence (High / Medium / Low)

Numeric model proven infeasible — Claude produces 10-15% variance on identical inputs and can't decompose correlated signals. Qualitative model with evidence-based rationale is more honest and more useful. See Section 05 for details.

Leadership Asked For

Explainability-to-Confidence Pipeline

Score → ISO clause → threshold → evidence → confidence → rationale. The "missing middle layer."

Our Approach

Full Chain — Adopted As-Is

This vision is exactly right. We build the complete explainability chain. The only change: confidence is qualitative (H/M/L + rationale) instead of a percentage.

Leadership Asked For

5-Table Database Architecture

compliance_frameworks, framework_versions, framework_controls, dimension_control_mappings, assessment_compliance_results.

Our Approach

6-Table with Criteria Versioning

Same 5 tables, plus a new interpretive_criteria table. This enables correcting/improving Guardian's criteria without creating phantom ISO versions. See Section 10 for details.

Leadership Asked For

Framework Versioning

Controls immutable per version. Old assessments keep their version reference.

Our Approach

Two-Level Versioning

We separate the ISO standard version (42001:2023) from Guardian's criteria version (guardian-iso42001-v1.0). This means we can fix a typo in our interpretive criteria without pretending ISO published a new edition.

Leadership Asked For

Interpretive Controls (IP Approach)

Guardian's own language referencing ISO clause numbers. No verbatim reproduction. CSA Group licensing as parallel track.

Our Approach

Adopted As-Is + Messaging Guidelines

Interpretive approach adopted exactly. We added strict messaging guidelines: Guardian uses "ISO-traceable" and "ISO-informed" language, never "ISO-compliant" or "ISO-certified." See Section 12.

Gaps We Discovered

Two of 10 dimensions have weak or no ISO coverage. ISO 42001 is industry-agnostic — it has no clinical/healthcare controls and minimal vendor viability controls. Rather than fabricate misleading mappings, these dimensions use Guardian's own healthcare-specific criteria and are clearly labeled as such in reports.
Coverage Gap

Clinical Risk

ISO 42001 contains no patient safety, clinical decision support, or healthcare workflow controls. This dimension relies entirely on Guardian's healthcare-specific criteria.

Future: IEC 62304 / ISO 14971 supplementation possible in Tier 2/3.

Coverage Gap

Vendor Viability

Only 3 generic third-party controls exist in ISO 42001. No financial stability, lock-in risk, data portability, or exit strategy coverage.

Future: ISO 27001 business continuity mapping possible in Tier 3+.

05 — Confidence Model: Why We Changed It

This is the largest deviation from leadership's original proposal. Here's why.

Original Proposal

4-Signal Weighted Formula

  • Evidence Specificity: 35%
  • Coverage Completeness: 30%
  • Response Consistency: 20%
  • Verifiability: 15%

Output: "72%" per dimension

Refined Approach

Qualitative + Rationale

  • High: Strong, verifiable evidence
  • Medium: Partial evidence, some gaps
  • Low: Vague or contradictory claims

Output: "Medium" + evidence-based explanation

Why the Numeric Model Doesn't Work

Finding 1: Variance Is Too High

When scoring identical vendor responses multiple times, Claude produces 10–15% different confidence values each run. A vendor might get "72%" one time and "58%" the next. A number that varies by 15% on the same input isn't a measurement — it's noise.

Finding 2: The 4 Signals Are Correlated, Not Independent

The formula treats specificity, coverage, consistency, and verifiability as independent inputs. They aren't. A vendor that gives vague answers (low specificity) also has low verifiability and low coverage. Claude can't decompose these into separate measurements because they're fundamentally entangled.

Finding 3: Claude Can't Count Coverage

"Coverage Completeness" requires counting what percentage of relevant ISO controls were addressed by vendor responses. This requires cross-referencing 20+ Q&A pairs against 30+ controls — a counting task that LLMs perform unreliably.

The qualitative model is more useful for decision-making. Leadership doesn't need "72% vs 68%." They need to know: "Is this score backed by solid evidence, or are we working with vague claims?" High / Medium / Low with a clear rationale answers that question directly.

Compliance Safeguard

The rationale string is mandatory, not optional. Every confidence level must cite the specific evidence and ISO references that justify it. A bare "Medium" with no explanation is rejected by the system. This ensures the qualitative model maintains audit defensibility.

12 — ISO Messaging Guidelines

Guardian is not a certification body. Precise language protects our credibility and avoids legal exposure.

Approved Language

Use These Terms

  • "ISO-traceable" — our criteria reference ISO clauses
  • "ISO-informed" — scoring considers ISO controls
  • "maps to ISO 42001 A.x.x.x" — specific reference
  • "aligned with" — directional, not absolute
Prohibited Language

Never Use These Terms

  • "ISO-compliant" — implies certification we can't grant
  • "ISO-certified" — only certification bodies certify
  • "meets ISO requirements" — implies conformity assessment
  • "ISO score" — no such concept exists
Why this matters: A single "ISO-compliant" in a report could undermine Guardian's credibility with auditors and create liability. These guidelines are enforced in prompt instructions, report templates, and will be automatically linted in Phase 2.
